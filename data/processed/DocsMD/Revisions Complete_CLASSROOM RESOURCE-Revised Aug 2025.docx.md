## Glossary on Terminology

Boys' disengagement from education is understood as a gendered phenomenon partly rooted in societal norms and concepts of masculinity, which can lead boys and young men to consciously or unconsciously to withdraw from education. Additional social, economic and cultural factors, in families, communities and schools, may also lead to boys' low engagement with education and contribute to school leaving/dropout. Disengagement from education can manifest in different forms such as lack of interest, poor participation, absenteeism, neglecting homework or exam preparation and ultimately, dropping out (UNESCO, 2022a).

Curriculum (Plural: Curricula) is an inventory of activities related to the design, organization and planning of an education or training action, including definition of learning objectives, content, methods (including assessment) and material. In simple terms, a description of what, why, how and when students should learn (UNESCO-IIEP, n.d.; UNESCO-IBE, 2013).

Disadvantage in education refers to the intersecting social, economic, cultural, or structural conditions that limit educational access, participation, and outcomes. Disadvantage can lead to particular groups (e.g. boys or girls) having lower enrolment, retention and completion rates, and poorer educational outcomes, including subject mastery and attainment.

Discrimination is an unfair treatment or arbitrary distinction based on a person's race, sex, religion, nationality, ethnic origin, sexual orientation, disability, age, language, social origin or other status (UNESCO, 2022b).

Drop-out or school leaving refers to leaving school before the completion of a given stage of education or leaving at some intermediate or non-terminal point in a cycle of schooling. It can be temporary or definitive and can be the result of an active decision made by the learner or the result of push and/or pull factors (based on UNESCO-IIEP, n.d.)

Educational space is a space where teaching and learning takes place. It can be inside or outside the school, physical or virtual. This can include classrooms, libraries, community spaces (e.g. youth centers), as well as e-learning platforms.

Gender refers to the culturally defined roles, attributes, behaviours, identities and expectations associated with being male or female in a given context. It shapes how individuals act and interact, and the distribution of power and resources in relationships and society. Gender can be expressed through male, female, and non-binary identities and may or may not be congruent with the sex assigned to an individual at their time of birth . (based on UNESCO, 2022b).

Gender-based violence refers to violence that results in, or is likely to result in, physical, sexual or psychological harm or suffering, against someone based on gender discrimination, gender role expectations and/or gender stereotypes, or based on the differential power status linked to gender (UNESCO, 2022b).

Gender equality in education means that the right to education of all learners is respected equally regardless of gender. Learners of all genders are given equal access to learning opportunities, resources and protections, and learners of all genders benefit from and are treated in education equally (based on UNESCO, 2019a).

Gender equality through education refers to education's key role in addressing the wider issue of gender equality. Educational institutions can promote equitable attitudes, changing the way people think about traditional gender roles and helping to build long-term sustainable change. And achieving equal outcomes for female, male and non-binary learners can help to empower all people to create better lives (UNESCO, 2019a).

Gender norms are understood as ideas about how men and women should behave: the expectations and standards that are set for each gender in different societies, cultures and communities. They are both context- and time-specific. People internalize these 'rules' at an early age, in the beginning of a cycle of gender socialization and stereotyping that continues for the rest of their lives. Gender norms thus not only become individuals' expectations of others, but also of themselves. Gender norms can be harmful, such as those that limit individuals' behaviours, opportunities, aspirations or relationships based on their gender, or that reinforce unequal power relations between genders (see also 'harmful and restrictive masculinities'). They can also be positive, when they promote equity, inclusion and shared power, for example, by encouraging people of all genders to pursue a full range of roles, express themselves freely, and share responsibilities (see also 'positive and inclusive masculinities'). The socialization process happening within educational institutions often replicates that of broader society and can contribute to the reproduction of social and gender norms (Marcus et al. 2024; UNICEF et al., 2019; Cookson et al., 2023; Stromquist, 2007).

Gender parity in education is an indicator of the ratio of girls to boys, women to men. Gender parity is reached when there is equal representation and participation of male and female learners in education. It is a useful indicator, but by itself does not measure gender equality (UNESCO, 2022b).

Gender-responsive means that strategies include evidence-based gender analysis which identifies and acknowledges existing gender differences, needs and inequalities. Activities at this level include specific policies and actions that address inequalities. Gender-responsive strategies, such as training in gender-responsive pedagogy, can be used as part of a system-wide, gender-transformative approach to education.

Gender-responsive pedagogy is understood as teaching and learning processes that pay attention to the specific learning needs of learners of different genders. In practical terms, this means that the learning materials, methodologies, content, learning activities, language use, classroom interaction, assessment and classroom set up respond to specific needs of learners of different genders (UNESCO-IIEP, n.d.).

Gender socialisation is the process through which individuals learn, internalize, and perform socially constructed gender norms, roles, and behaviours. This occurs through interactions with key agents of socialization, such as family, peers, the media, and social institutions. For example, boys learn and internalize how 'real men' should be through gender socialization (John et al., 2017).

Gender stereotypes are simplistic generalizations about the gender attributes, differences and roles of girls, women, boys and men and people of other genders (UN Women, 2025).

A gender-transformative approach entails addressing the underlying causes of gender inequalities. It includes implementing policies and initiatives which not only address the different needs, aspirations, capacities and contributions of girls, boys, women, men and non-binary people, but also challenge existing and discriminatory policies and practices, creating radical change. A gender-transformative approach to education is one that encompasses policy, programming and interventions to create opportunities to actively challenge gender norms and wider inequalities. This includes promoting gender equality through curricular and teaching reforms (UNESCO, 2022a).

Harmful and restrictive masculinities are linked to hegemonic gender norms which suggest that boys and men should conform to a 'right' or 'ideal' way to be a man or a boy (see 'hegemonic masculinity'). This 'ideal' places pressure on boys and men to conform to certain expectations, which often include being self-sufficient, acting tough and using aggression to solve conflict. It is constructed in opposition to femininities and/or marginalized masculinities. Such ideas and practices of masculinity are harmful to girls, women and non-binary people as they uphold men's privilege over women and non-binary people. They are also harmful to boys and men, who are regulated by rigid expectations of manhood that restrict their freedom to express emotions, seek help, or deviate from dominant masculine norms. Harmful and restrictive masculinities can undermine boys' motivation and learning, because they often position academic effort and aspiration as incompatible with being a 'real' boy or man, affecting boys' motivation to learn (Heilman et al., 2017; UNESCO, 2022a; UNESCO-IBE, 2025 WHO, 2019).

Hegemonic masculinity is the culturally dominant form of masculinity that is commonly upheld as the 'right' or 'ideal' way to be a man or a boy. In many contexts it positions men and boys as superior to women and girls and people of other gender identities, with negative consequences for people of all genders (Connell, 2005; Marcus et al., 2024).

Homophobia refers to fear, discomfort, intolerance or hatred of homosexuality or sexually diverse people (UNESCO, 2015).

Inclusive education is the process of strengthening the capacity of the education system to reach out to and include all students (UNESCO, 2022b).

LGBTIQ+ stands for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, intersex and queer/questioning people, with the '+' symbol acknowledging those who identify as gender-diverse and who use additional terminologies. Lesbian refers to a woman who is emotionally, romantically, or sexually attracted to other women. Gay refers to a person, often understood to be a man, who is emotionally, romantically, or sexually attracted to people of the same gender. Bisexual refers to a person who is emotionally, romantically, or sexually attracted to more than one gender. Transgender refers to a person whose gender identity and/or expression differs from the sex they were assigned at birth. Intersex refers to a person born with physical sex characteristics (such as chromosomes, sexual anatomy, or reproductive organs) that do not fit typical definitions of male or female. Queer is often used as an umbrella term for sexual and gender diversity. Questioning refers to a person who is exploring or uncertain about their sexual orientation or gender identity (based on UNESCO, 2020; UNESCO, 2019b; UNESCO, 2016).

Non-binary is an umbrella term that refers to a gender identity outside of the male-female gender binary. This can include people with a specific gender identity other than man or woman, people who identify as two or more genders, and people who do not identify with any gender (based on UNHCR, 2024).

Positive and healthy masculinities emphasize the constructive aspects of masculine identities. They serve as a counterpoint to harmful and restrictive gender norms that portray men and boys as potential perpetrators or aggressors and focus solely on the harm they cause to themselves and others. It emphasises that all boys and men should be free to express diverse expressions of positive masculinities without fear of exclusion, ridicule or violence. Positive and healthy masculinities refer to alternative expressions of 'hegemonic masculinity' (see above) that align with egalitarian values and promote health and well-being. Examples include committed fatherhood and partnership, caring and intimacy, and help-seeking behaviour. The use of the plural term 'masculinities' underscores that there are many positive ways to be a boy or man. It also recognizes that masculine (and feminine) gender norms differ across cultures and contexts and change over time. Promoting positive and healthy masculinities is central in efforts to shift gender norms that harm boys and men and the people around them (based on UNESCO, 2014; UN Women, 2016). Sex: Refers to the biological characteristics that distinguish males, females and intersex individuals (UNESCO, 2022b). Sex characteristics are physical or biological features. Sexual orientation refers to a person's capacity for profound emotional and sexual attraction to, and intimate and sexual relations with, individuals of a different gender, the same gender or more than one gender (UNESCO, 2019b; UNESCO, 2016). Social norms refer to rules, often informal and implicit, that guide how people are expected to behave in a group or society. People are often rewarded for conforming to these norms and punished for deviating from them (Marcus et al., 2024). Teaching and learning materials are understood as tools and resources used by educators to support their lesson planning and delivery, and by students in their learning process. They include a wide range of products such as textbooks, workbooks, visual aids, storybooks, digital/online resources, classroom handouts and assessments (UNESCO-IIEP, n.d.). A whole-school approach considers the entire school ecosystem rather than its individual parts when working with students and teachers to achieve change, for example towards gender equality. Common elements of a whole-school approach are policies, teacher training, pedagogies, environments and engaging with parents and caregivers (Marcus et al., 2024; Plan International et al., 2021). ## Overview

The Classroom Lesson Plans for Learners on Masculinities and Gender Equality was developed and informed by key recommendations from UNESCO's report, Leave No Child Behind: Global Report on Boy's Disengagement from Education . Boys' disengagement from education is caused by multiple factors that lead boys and young men to withdraw from education, either consciously or unconsciously. While there are many driving factors behind boy's disengagement, findings from the report highlight that among them, social and gender norms pose a significant obstacle to boys in their pursuit of education and lifelong learning.

The Classroom Lesson Plans aim to provide educators with a gender-transformative and asset-based approach to working with boys in formal educational spaces. Designed to be led and facilitated by educators, these lessons aim to work with learners to challenge the harmful gender norms driving boys' disengagement in the classroom and support the creation of more inclusive learning environments for creating greater gender equality for all.

## Boys' Disengagement from Education and the Role of Educators

Globally, boys' disengagement from school is driven by social, economic, and cultural factors, including harmful gender norms - society's unwritten rules about how men and women should be. The school environment and other educational factors such as rigid teaching methods and harsh discipline, and rigid gender norms in school also play a large role in driving boys' disengagement. Specifically, the factors that drive boys' disengagement from education vary by setting, but can include 1 :

- Economic Factors . In their families, boys can face pressure to be economic providers forcing them to prioritize household financial well-being over schooling. In other places, poor families may struggle with food insecurity, inadequate health care and community violence, all of which negatively impact children's ability to attend school regularly.
- Example: Boys in lower secondary schools in Jamaica often leave school to contribute to household income or due to early employment opportunities (Stampini et al., 2018; UNESCO, 2022c).
- Social Factors . For example, influences from peers to engage in risk-taking behavior such as substance abuse, peer violence, and addiction to gaming. Boys are also vulnerable to harmful influences from social media that promote stereotypical versions of manhood. All of this can undermine their focus on education. Families and communities can contribute to boys' disengagement from education by not providing academic support or encouragement for reasons such as they view schooling as less important than being in paid employment.
- Educational Factors . Rigid teaching methods that leave little room for flexibility, creativity, or adaptation to students' needs, an emphasis on memorization versus critical thinking, and the lack of inclusive or learner-centered programs can make boys' disengagement worse, alongside limited safe spaces, and insufficient mental health support (e.g. psychological counseling). In addition, boys can also face corporal punishment and other forms of violence from their educators.
- Rigid Social and Gender Norms. Harmful and restrictive masculine norms both inside and outside the classroom are barriers to boys' engagement in education. Increasingly, academic achievement is seen as 'feminine,' discouraging boys from reading and engaging fully in the classroom.
- Examples from two countries: Boys interested in reading are mocked in Chile as reading is viewed as a feminine activity (Espinoza and Strasser 2020); Older boys in the UK show decreasing interest in reading, especially after primary school (Picton &amp; Clark, 2019; OECD, 2015).
- Policy Factors . Few policies address boys' disengagement from education and those that do exist are limited in approach and reach, and may even have an adverse impact on boy's engagement with formal education.
- Example: Zero-tolerance policies in the United States that require strict, predetermined punishments such as expulsion or suspension have led to the exclusion of a huge number of African American boys involved in discipline issues (Bell 2015; Johnson and Johnson 2023).

There are many long-term costs to boys and wider society when they are not engaged in education. These include 2 :

- Poorer economic outcomes , such as lower salaries since higher educational attainment often translate into acquiring skills that are better remunerated. 3
- Poorer health and wellbeing . Research shows men with higher educational attainment have better health outcomes, while a lack of education and employment opportunities can push men into poverty and to have poorer health outcomes.
- Worse gender attitudes and relationships . Better educated men are more likely to support policies that promote gender equality and protect women from discrimination, while poorer gender attitudes are linked with men's perpetration violence against intimate partners, and unequal caregiving roles in the household. 4

While school-level factors such as harsh discipline, unequal gender norms, and experiences of peer violence can discourage boys from achieving their educational goals, educators and the educational institutions where they work play a crucial role in creating inclusive school environments where boys can thrive . This resource, alongside other Lifting Barriers Global Resources , highlights ways that educators can strengthen student-teacher relationships, create opportunities to learn from peers, and promote a sense of inclusiveness and fairness. All of this contributes to boys' sense of belonging in school and overall sense of happiness (see Box. UNESCO's Happy Schools Initiative).

## UNESCO's Happy Schools Initiative

This resource is equally important in complementing UNESCO's global Happy Schools Initiative, which urges education authorities to prioritize happiness in education and expand joyful learning practices from schools to policy levels, guided by UNESCO's global Happy Schools framework. The model comprises four pillars people, process, place, and principles - and 12 high-level criteria for integrating happiness into education systems. Building Happy Schools recognizes that unequal social and gender norms can lead to bias, stigma, discrimination, and violence - all of which contribute to decreased well-being and learning. This resource, alongside the other Lifting Barriers resources, aim to provide teachers with tools to create more inclusive spaces that support boys' educational achievement, alongside girls and nonbinary learners.

## Purpose of the Classroom Lesson Plans for Learners and Main Objectives

This Classroom Resource includes 10 gender-transformative , interactive, and participatory Lesson Plans that can be done with boys and their peers, regardless of their gender identity. Taking a gender-transformative approach to education means working with boys and their peers to critically reflect on and challenge the root causes of gender inequality, such as the harmful gender attitudes and behaviors that perpetuate boys' disengagement from the classroom, and encouraging them to discover and embrace more peaceful and equitable alternatives. These Lesson Plans also take an asset-based approach to engaging boys. Rather than treating them as problems to be fixed, it recognizes their strengths, leadership potential, and capacity for growth. The purpose of these Lesson Plans is to provide guidance to educators on how to nurture boys' existing sense of care, empathy for others, sense of justice and responsibility, while also helping them to develop skills that benefit both their personal development and educational attainment.

<!-- image -->

Equally important, this guide emphasizes the link between gender equality and educational engagement . Research shows that harmful and restrictive masculine norms can contribute to school disengagement, low academic performance, and dropout rates among boys. By addressing these norms and promoting positive identities, the Classroom Lesson Plans help foster an environment where boys feel supported in their learning, motivated to succeed academically, and encouraged to see education as a key part of their future.

By using these Classroom Lesson Plans, educators will help all learners, especially boys, to:

1. Reflect critically on how gender norms and stereotypes influence their experiences and attitudes toward learning, and imagine more equitable possibilities;
2. Strengthen their collective ability to challenge power imbalances and foster a more respectful and inclusive school environment;
3. Develop more positive peer relationships that are supportive of educational success;
4. Learn how to normalize the healthy expression of emotions and help-seeking behavior, especially in school.

## How To Use These Lesson Plans

## Who are these Lesson Plans for?

These lesson plans are for educators (e.g. teachers, teacher trainers, teaching assistants) working with upper primary school learners (roughly, ages 9 to 12 years old). The ages of upper primary students vary slightly across different countries, but generally, 'upper primary' refers to the last few years of primary education before transitioning to lower secondary school. This is because globally, research shows that, in many places, boys are more likely than girls to face difficulties in the transition between upper primary to secondary school. 5 Therefore, objectives, activities, and discussion questions are mostly centered on the gender-specific challenges and opportunities boys have in their pursuit of education.

However, all learners should be enabled to challenge rigid gender norms and learn from educational materials that are gender-transformative. This means educators can use these lesson plans with all learners, regardless of their gender identity, to promote a positive learning culture inclusive and free from stereotypes. Educators and their educational institutions should consider implementing these Lesson Plans alongside other gender equality initiatives such as ones focused on girls' education (see A Whole School Approach further below). Educators can also adapt sessions to broaden reflection on how new insights, skills, and awareness can benefit every learners' ability to succeed in education .

## Mixed Gender vs. Separated Groups

These Lesson Plans are designed to be used with both girls and boys in mixed gender spaces. However, some learners benefit more from activities and group reflections when they are separated into smaller gender-segregated groups. Separate spaces allow for open sharing, while mixed gender discussions can help build understanding.  Educators managing large class sizes may find it challenging to split students by gender for deeper reflection on sensitive topics. Depending on the context, these discussions can take place by, for example, splitting learners into smaller gender-specific groups. Even without physical separation, think of ways to create opportunities for reflection through carefully structured activities that encourage all students to engage thoughtfully and respectfully.

## Where should these Lesson Plans be implemented?

These Lesson Plans should be implemented with groups of learners in an educational setting either in a physical space, such as a classroom, or in a virtual learning space. They can also be used in non-formal education settings such as youth community centers that provide mentorship, tutoring, or life skills programming. Educators can also further reinforce learning and create broader enabling environments for boys' educational attainment by incorporating the key messages from the lessons outside the learning space. For example, learners and educators can work together to create school campaigns, activities during annual school days, and organize assemblies centered on the main themes of these lessons to encourage school-wide reflection and action.

## What Is My Role in these Lesson Plans?

Your role in these Lesson Plans is to create a safe environment, listen openly, and encourage more discussion among your learners. You do not have to necessarily be a subject matter 'expert,' but in addition to having engaged in gender training organized by your educational institution, it is important to read through and do the exercises contained in the Educators' Guide , a resource under the Lifting Barriers project. The Educators' Guide provides educators with strategies to create gender-equitable learning environments that support boys' growth and academic success, alongside their peers. Above all, it is important that you learn about how gender and norms around masculinities influence boys' disengagement in the classroom, as well as how personal biases and experiences influence your teaching practices and ways of communication.

Many of the activities in these Lesson Plans ask learners to share sensitive life experiences, such as struggling in school or issues with friends. In line with a trauma-informed approach, you can help create a safe and supportive environment by modeling this behavior with small personal experiences of your own, or by preparing other realistic examples. A trauma-informed approach means recognizing that some learners may carry difficult past experiences and ensuring classroom activities do not re-trigger or shame them. For example, you might:

- Give students the option to 'pass' if they are not comfortable sharing.
- Use hypothetical or role-play scenarios instead of requiring personal disclosures.

When boys are engaged in critical discussions about what it means to be a man or that ask them to be vulnerable by sharing personal experiences of struggle, this might be the first time they are discussing such topics. Doing so in front of their peers can be even more intimidating, especially in a mixed gender setting. Here are some recommendations to implement the Lesson Plans in ways that create safer and more accepting spaces for boys and their peers. Recommendations for Engaging Boys:

## A Whole School Approach

Gender equality-focused efforts to address boys' disengagement from education must be approached thoughtfully and in conjunction with wider school mandates and initiatives. They should also be communicated transparently with parents and caregivers. In short, these Lesson Plans should not be implemented in isolation but rather as part of a whole school approach to promote gender equality . According to UNESCO, a whole school approach involves addressing the needs of learners, staff and the wider community, not only within the curriculum, but across the whole-school and learning environment. 6 In the context of addressing boys' disengagement in schools, this can mean:

- -Working with key educational allies and networks to advocate for national and local education policies mandating gender equality education in the classroom;
- -Developing a school-wide strategy, action plans, and school policies driven by school leadership committed to creating an enabling environment for all learners, especially boys, to thrive.
- -Working with school leadership to identify gender 'champions' within schools to help embed ideas and practice, and to adapt the Lesson Plans so they fit within established curricula standards.
- -Training school leaders and professional teacher networks, such as teachers' unions, connected with the educational institution, on the effects of harmful and restrictive masculine norms on boys' educational attainment, and using the Lifting Barriers Resources such as the Educator's Guide and Lesson Plans to implement gender-transformative education effectively.
- -Briefing primary caregivers (e.g. parents/guardians) on the implementation of boys' engagement in education initiatives, and encouraging them to support their child at home wherever possible to ensure continued engagement and positive reinforcement. The Lifting Barriers, Parents Guide , provides structured guidance on how to run parent and caregiver meetings to both present the initiative on boys' education as well as how to share the impacts.

## Tips on Managing Resistance with Boys

Defensiveness and backlash: Critical discussions about the relationship between gender, masculinity, power, privilege and gender-based discrimination (e.g. prejudice and hostility towards women and girls) can elicit anger, defensiveness and resistance within some adolescent boys. Rather than viewing angry, resistant and defensive responses as 'problems', see them more as opportunities to engage constructively with boys who are in a process of critically thinking about and working through their inherited beliefs about gender and masculinity. Give boys space to speak about any grievances, frustrations or discomfort they feel about gender-related issues.

Listen to these feelings, but do not validate them or agree with discriminatory or hateful comments. Acknowledge that not all boys or men experience power and privilege in the same way-factors like class, race, sexuality, (dis)abilities and ethnicity can shape the opportunities and challenges they face. These differences matter and can influence how boys relate to conversations about gender and fairness. Point out that boys and men can play an active role in building a culture that supports healthy, respectful and equitable social relationships by helping to tackle and prevent harmful practices, such as gender-based discrimination, racism, classism and so forth.

Discriminatory and hateful comments: Educators should be mindful that they may hear discriminatory and hateful comments when discussing harmful forms of masculinity. Listen to, but do not validate, harmful or discriminatory comments. A range of appropriate and proportionate responses can be used to deal with these responses, such as friendly reminders to the whole group to respect the 'group agreement'. However, try not to shut down or ban specific topics about masculinity from discussion as this may lead boys to feel that their views, concerns and experiences are not being heard. Instead, ask questions that aim to promote critical thinking e.g., 'How would you feel if someone you care for was treated this way?'. Asking such questions can also cultivate empathy for those who have been subjected to such discrimination, which can help participants to understand their negative impacts.

Personal boundaries and self-care: If you find yourself emotionally affected or triggered by resistance or difficult comments from learners, it's okay to pause and seek support. You can ask for a break, reach out to a colleague, or share (if appropriate) with students that the conversation is challenging for you. See if you can model respectful disagreement.

## Customizing these Lesson Plans to Your Setting

These Lesson Plans provide a structured approach to addressing boys' disengagement from education in fun and participatory ways. However, you may find that some activities are inappropriate for your setting or learners. It will be important to tailor the content of your groups to your context. Use the following questions to inform how you will adapt these Lessons:

- What are the specific challenges boys face with regard to engaging in education?
- What specific skills and support do boys and their peers need to increase their engagement in education?
- What dominant cultural narratives about gender and masculinity are shaping boys' behaviors and attitudes in this context? (For example: narratives centered on militarism, sports dominance, economic success, or sexual conquest.)
- How do local power dynamics related to race, class, caste, ability, ethnicity, or migration status intersect with gender and masculinity to shape boys' experiences in and out of school?
- Are there community leaders, cultural practices, or local stories that could help affirm more liberatory and diverse models of masculinity for boys?
- What kinds of games and popular media are boys and their peers interested in and how can these be incorporated into these lessons?

Depending on the time and resources your educational institution has, you may even want to have an informal discussion with boys and their peers using questions like the above to better understand their needs and challenges.

## Lesson 2: What is Gender?

## Teaching Tip Sheet

The purpose of this Lesson is to help learners understand how gender socialization begins from the time we are born, and how rigid gender expectations influence and can harm girls and boys differently. Keep these Guiding Ideas in mind as you facilitate these activities:

## Guiding Ideas

- Boys often experience conflicting societal pressures to think and act in strict or narrow definitions of what it means to be a man.
- These are known as gender norms , or society's traditional rules for how men and women should think, act, and behave.
- Gender norms often perpetuate gender inequality where - as a whole - women and girls have less power and privilege in society than men and boys (e.g. boys who have freedom to play outside with friends, while girls must stay home to take care of the household chores). However, though boys and men-as a whole-have more power and privilege, they also experience many restrictions and harm due to these same gender norms.
- Gender norms play a large role in driving boys' disengagement from education. Boys are less likely to transition to secondary school, are more likely to be disciplined harshly in school, and experience physical violence than girls, among other factors. Boys internalize these rules from an early age through gender socialization . Gender socialization is a process during which people learn to 'be' their gender through interacting with their family members, their peers, the media, and other sources around them.
- As you conduct this Lesson, acknowledge the strengths that already exist within each boy (an asset-based approach) as opposed to focusing on what you think they lack. Tap into their creativity, energy and resources so that each boy feels supported to maximize their potential.

## Activity 1 : A Boy's Story - Part 1 (20 minutes)

1. Tell them you will read a story about a boy. Say, 'Let's give him a name.' Ask learners to pick a name appropriate for the story.
2. Read the following story to your learners:

Once upon a time, a boy named [NAME] was born. He was born needing love and attention from those around him. When he cried, his mother would pick him up from his crib and sing him sweet songs until he fell asleep. Other times, his father would take him in his arms, rock him gently, and give him lots of kisses all over his face.

3. Ask them, how do you think this boy feels when his parents comfort him? Does he feel scared? Or does he feel safe? Why?
4. Continue reading the story:

As the boy grew a little older, he started school. When it was time to play outside, the boy would race his friends to see who could run the fastest. Some of the teachers told the girls to stop running or they would get dirty. So the girls stopped running.

5. Ask the learners:
- What happened to the girls when they raced with the boys when it was time to play? How do you think the girls felt?
6. Continue reading the story:

The next day, the Boy fell down as he started racing his friends. His knee hurt so much he started to cry. His teacher ran over to him and instead of giving him comfort, the teacher told the boy that he wasn't a baby anymore. It was time for him to 'be a man'. So his teacher brushed the dust off his pants and told him to stop crying. So the Boy did.

7. Ask the learners:
- How do you think the boy felt when he hurt himself? How did he feel when his teacher told him not to cry? Have you ever felt like this?
- What other messages do boys receive about how to 'be a man'?
8. Make the following points below and present the definitions. These definitions can be written on the chalk board or on a flipchart paper.
- As we grow up, we receive all kinds of messages from society (e.g. our parents, teachers, the media) about how girls and boys should act and think according to their gender .
- 'Gender' is a word to describe the roles, responsibilities, and qualities (e.g. how you should look, act, think) linked with being, or being seen as a man/boy or woman/girl.
- Throughout our lives, we get messages about how 'men/boys' and 'women/girls' should be. This includes thinking that certain activities are 'natural' or 'normal' for people based on their gender.

'Gender roles' are how boys and girls are expected to act . For example, beliefs that boys and men should be the ones to earn the money, while girls and women stay at home to take care of household chores and children.

- Gender is not biological or natural, but is learned from families, friends, in schools and our communities. Being our gender is something we get to explore and decide for ourselves - everyone can do it their own way.
9. Say that they will discuss more about gender in the next activities.

## Lesson 3: Gender Stereotypes Teaching Tip Sheet

The purpose of this Lesson is to further explore gender stereotypes and promote empathy amongst students in the classroom. Like it can for girls, gender stereotypes can negatively impact boys' confidence and motivation to do well in school. For example, some parents and teachers believe that girls are naturally better readers and are more inclined to enjoy reading, which can discourage boys from developing the joy of reading. According to UNESCO, 'these stereotypes can have long-lasting effects, as boys internalize these beliefs and become less likely to engage in reading and other academic activities 7 .' There are many benefits to boys when learning environments are free from gender stereotypes such as:

- Improved academic performance - boys would be encouraged to pursue subjects they excel at, without being limited by gender stereotypes.
- Reduced bullying - Boys would feel more accepted and feel less pressure to solve problems with violence.
- Better mental health - Boys would feel higher self-esteem and less likely to experience mental health issues such as with body image.
- More diverse career choices - Boys could be encouraged to explore a wider range of subjects and careers without being limited by gender stereotypes.

Throughout this Lesson, encourage your learners (and yourself!) to challenge gender stereotypes and emphasize the benefits of learning in an environment where they would not feel bound by its restrictions.

## Lesson 4: Learning About Masculinities Teaching Tip Sheet

The purpose of this session is to recognize the challenges boys and girls face in trying to fulfill societal expectations about how to be a 'real man' and 'real woman', understand the negative consequences, and identify positive benefits of becoming more genuine versions of who they would like themselves to be.

On a daily basis, boys and young men receive many restrictive and sometimes contradictory messages on how to be a 'real man.' It is important to understand the pressures boys feel to conform to these unrealistic expectations and the consequences they feel they may face such as bullying, teasing, and even social isolation. At the same time, these rigid expectations have consequences on their education and wider well-being, as described in earlier sections. Learning about different types and representations of men and masculinities (qualities of being a man), can help them to understand the positive and negative ways in which harmful definitions of manhood can impact education and their lives more broadly.

## Lesson 4: Learning About Masculinities

## (adapted from Equimundo resources)

## Objective

The purpose of this session is to recognize the challenges boys and girls face in trying to fulfill societal expectations about how to be a 'real man' and 'real woman', understand the negative consequences, and identify positive benefits of becoming more genuine versions of who they would like themselves to be.

## Total Lesson Plan Time: 1 hour

Activity 1: The Man Box (20 minutes)

Activity 2: The Woman Box (20 minutes)

Activity 3: The Human Space (20 minutes)

## Recommended Materials

- Flipchart paper, or another writeable surface
- Colored tape
- Sticky notes or small pieces of paper
- Markers or something to write with

## Activity 1 : The Man Box (20 minutes)

1. Explain that this activity will focus on gender stereotypes about masculinity - what it means to be a man and how these stereotypes affect boys.
2. Ask the group, 'What are the words or images that come into your mind when people tell you, 'Act like a man!''
3. Encourage them to shout out the answers. Write them down until you get a long list.
4. Say, 'Our society has many expectations for how boys should think and act, especially as they grow older. Some of these expectations are good, but others can be harmful for boys and those they care about.'
5. Break the learners into small groups of 5 - 7 people.
6. Give each small group 10 small pieces of paper, tape and a dark-colored marker.
7. Tell the groups that they will have 3 to 5 minutes to write one message or word on each piece of paper that boys hear every day about how to be a 'real man'. They can use the long list you made at the start of the activity.
8. As they come up with the messages, they must tape the pieces of paper on their bodies (or on just one group member) until they are covered. Whoever has the most pieces of paper wins.
9. As they do the activity, draw a large box on the floor with tape or place a carton large enough to step into on the floor.
10. After 3 to 5 minutes, ask the small groups to come back together and look at the box.
11. Say, 'the box you see in the middle is called 'The Man Box'. The messages young men learn can force them into The Man Box - our society, or culture's, rules for how to be a 'real man'.'
12. Ask one group to step into 'The Man Box' to share the top 3-4 messages they hear. The winning group (the group that has the most pieces of paper) gets to go first. After they present, other groups can contribute messages that haven't yet been shared.
13. Ask everyone to take off the labels / pieces of paper from their bodies and sit back down.
14. Ask, 'how did it feel to do this activity?'
15. Say, it can feel like a relief to realize that many of us dislike getting these kinds of messages. We are all experiencing the same pressures to live up to gender stereotypes .
16. Ask, 'Where do we hear these messages for how to be a man? Who says these messages?'
17. Say that:
- Boys face a lot of pressure from family (e.g. to drop out of school to earn money and support the family), peer pressure from their male friends (e.g. to bully others, not focus on school), from social media influencers (e.g. perpetuate harmful stereotypes), and other places to act like a 'real' man.
- Many of these beliefs can limit boys' potential to succeed not only in school, but in life.
18. Ask, what are the consequences when boys feel forced to fit into this Man Box? For example, they can feel sad, angry, or depressed; it can affect their ability to focus on school.
19. Brainstorm the benefits of stepping outside the Man Box. What are the positive things that would happen if boys decided they did not want to live in this box? For example, they would feel free to express their feelings, or be involved in activities that they are interested in without worrying about being teased.
20. After the activity is over, save the work learners did to make it easier to review at the beginning of the next activity. You can re-create the Man Box on a separate flipchart paper and re-tape the messages or words inside the box.

## Activity 2 : The Woman Box (20 minutes)

21. If you are doing this activity on a different day, ask learners what they remember about the Man Box. Use the Man Box flipchart or sticky notes you saved to help them remember.
22. Explain that this activity will focus on gender stereotypes about femininity - what it means to be a woman and how these stereotypes affect girls.
23. Ask the group, 'What are the words or images that come into your mind when people tell you, 'Act like a woman!''
24. Encourage them to shout out the answers. Write them down until you get a long list.
25. Say, 'Our society has many expectations for how girls and young women should think and act, especially as they grow older. Some of these expectations are good, but others can be harmful for girls and those they care about.'
26. Break the learners into small groups of 5 - 7 people.
27. Give each small group 10 small pieces of paper, tape and a dark-colored marker.
28. Tell the groups that they will have 3 to 5 minutes to write one message or word on each piece of paper that girls hear every day about how to be a 'real woman'. They can use the long list you made at the start of the activity.
29. As they come up with the messages, they must tape the pieces of paper on their bodies (or on just one group member) until they are covered. Whoever has the most pieces of paper wins.
30. As they do the activity, draw a large box on the floor with tape.
31. After 3 to 5 minutes, ask the small groups to come back together and form a circle around the box you have drawn on the floor.
32. The box you see in the middle is called 'The Woman Box'. The messages young girls learn can force them into The Woman Box - our society, or culture's, rules for how to be a 'real woman'.
33. Ask each group to take turns stepping into 'The Woman Box' to share the top 3-4 messages they hear. The winning group (the group that has the most pieces of paper) gets to go first. Every group after should share messages that previous groups have not yet shared.
34. Once every group has gone, ask everyone to take off the labels / pieces of paper from their bodies and sit down in a circle.
35. Ask, 'How did it feel to do this activity?'
36. Say, like it is for boys, it can feel like a relief to realize that many girls dislike getting these kinds of messages. Many women and girls are experiencing the same pressures to live up to gender stereotypes .
37. Ask, the boys, did you learn anything that you were surprised about regarding the messages girls receive?
38. Say that like boys, girls also feel pressure from family (e.g. to spend more time on house chores than school work), peers (e.g. teasing, pressure to be quiet), and other places to act like a 'real' woman. Many of these beliefs can limit girls' potential to succeed not only in school, but in life.
39. Brainstorm the benefits of stepping outside the Woman Box. What are the positive things that would happen to girls if they decided they did not want to live in this box?
40. After the activity is over, save the work learners did to make it easier to review at the beginning of the next activity. You can re-create the Woman Box on a separate flipchart paper and re-tape the messages or words inside the box.

## Activity 3 : The Human Space (20 minutes)

41. If you are doing this activity on a different day, ask learners what they remember about the Man Box and Woman Box. Use the Man and Woman Boxes you saved to help them remember.
42. Draw a big box on a flipchart paper or on a visible writing surface like a chalkboard. Inside, write 'My Human Space.'
43. Tell everyone to imagine that there was no Man or Woman Box. In other words, no pressure from society on how to be a man or woman. Think of a world where you were expected to be simply human and to treat others as equals.
44. Ask them what types of qualities they would like to see in this 'Human Space' what messages or qualities would they like to have or hear from others about how to be human?
45. Write down the qualities as your learners shout them out or raise their hands.

Tip: Alternatively, you can make this an individual activity. Pass out paper and pencils and ask them to draw a large box on the paper called 'My Human Space.' Tell them to write inside all the qualities they would like to have or hear from others about how to be a human. After 5 minutes, you can ask a few learners to share their responses with the rest of the group.

46. Ask the group the following reflection questions:
- How can the positive qualities inside your Human Box help you in school? How about in your life more broadly?
- What help might you need from others in your life to live out these qualities in the Human Space?

## 47. End with the Key Message

A person, regardless of their gender or sex, can be any combination of characteristics inside the Human Space. Every person should be able to decide for themselves the kind of human they want to be. It should not be forced upon us because of our gender.

## Lesson 5: Succeeding In School Teaching Tip Sheet

The purpose of this session is to support boys and their peers by building their time management skills to have more time to both study and have fun. Unlike other Lessons, this is not purely about gender and masculinities. It is a lesson to help boys, girls, and their peers build time management skills. For this reason, this lesson can be done at any point in the school year.

Boys often report feeling overwhelmed once they perceive that they have fallen behind their peers and believe they are unable to catch up. This can form patterns of behavior where boys perceive that higher education is unnecessary or too challenging to pursue. Educators like you can intervene early by encouraging them to develop good practices. Boys respond well to tailored practical solutions that will help them to overcome barriers to their learning.

This session focuses on helping them to develop a daily and weekly schedule to better manage their time. However, if you identify different roadblocks to boys' education, adapt the session to build boys' skills to overcome that challenge. For example, boys may have issues with regular school attendance, or in finding the motivation to complete homework by setting deadlines. In your adaptation, keep the learning light, fun, and offer lots of encouragement. In addition, link boys with other learning support services such as academic counselors and tutors. You can even invite a young man enrolled in university to your classroom to talk about their experiences, what they did to get enrolled, and more.

1. Explain that this activity will focus on setting goals.
2. Ask the learners, 'What is a goal?'
3. Say: A goal is something you want to achieve.
4. Say that everyone wishes to have goals such as finishing your grade with good marks, or make new friends in school this year. To do well in school, it is important to have goals because it helps us to develop a plan we can follow. It is something you want to achieve or accomplish in a specific amount of time like in a month, several months, or a year.
5. Ask the learners to lie down or sit quietly on the ground. Tell them to close their eyes and take a deep breath.
6. Tell them: Imagine themselves in the next 1 to 2 years.

7. Tell them to open their eyes.
8. Tell them to get out a piece of paper and a pen or pencil for writing or drawing.
9. Explain that they will write down or draw what is their goal for school in the next 1 to 2 years. They should title the paper: 'My School Goal.' Optional: Pass out art supplies such as colored paper, paint, markers, and crayons to each learner.

13. If you have the space, you can have the learners tape up their goals on a wall where they can see them.

## Activity 2 : Want To's and Have To's (20 minutes)

1. Remind the learners that they learned about setting goals in the last activity. Say that in this activity they will talk about the things they can do every day to help achieve those goals.
2. Ask the learners to think about all the fun things they do in a day, from when they wake up to when they go to sleep. Remind them to also think of fun things like talking with friends, phone calls/chats with family members, and volunteering in the community.
3. After thinking silently to themselves for 1 minute, ask them to shout out their ideas to you.
4. As they share, write what they tell you on flipchart paper or another visible surface like a chalkboard.
5. Thank them for sharing the things they do each day. Say it is good to have time to do fun things like talking with friends and playing games. These are 'WANT TO's.' But we also have responsibilities that are important to help us do well in school, keep ourselves healthy, and help others. These are 'HAVE TO's'. For example, help clean up at home, do our school work, eat, and sleep.
6. Say that every day they have to choose between activities they want to do and activities they have to do.

'WANT TO's' are activities you want to do because you enjoy them.

'HAVE TO's' are activities you must do because they are necessary or important, even if you don't like them or want to do them.

7. Look at the activities on the flipchart. Let's now decide which ones you WANT TO DO and which ones you HAVE TO DO.
8. Go through the list of activities from the flipchart and ask the entire group to say which activities they think are WANT TO's or HAVE TO's. You may call on volunteers or ask the boys to shout out answers.
9. Mark them with two different colors to show the difference between WANT TO's (one color) and HAVE TO's (second color).
10. When the learners disagree, ask them to explain why the activity is either a WANT TO or HAVE TO, and ask the group to make a decision together. Mark the activity with the appropriate color.
11. Emphasize that WANT TO's are important because they make us happy. However, it is important to balance the WANT TO's with the HAVE TO's so that we can achieve our school goals and become successful in life.

## Teaching Tip Sheet

The main purpose of this lesson is to explore with boys' and their peers' their feelings of having power and feeling powerless, and its connections to bullying. Bullying has been defined as 'unwanted, aggressive behaviour among school-aged children that involves a real or perceived imbalance of power. The behaviour is repeated, or has the potential to be repeated, over time'. 9 Bullying is a form of school violence and can overlap with many other forms of violence such as corporal punishment, sexual abuse, assault, cyberbullying, physical fighting, and psychological violence such as insults and social exclusion.

Both boys and girls experience bullying, however the types of bullying they experience may differ based on their gender. Research shows that boys are more likely to be exposed to physical bullying and to physical violence in general, while girls are more likely to report experiencing psychological and sexual violence. Boys are also more likely to experience corporal punishment in school. Students who are perceived not to conform to prevailing sexual and gender norms, including those who are lesbian, gay,  bisexual or transgender (LGBT), are also more vulnerable to many types of school bullying. 10

There are many drivers behind school violence and bullying and they include unequal and restrictive gender and social norms, and wider contextual and structural factors. Much school violence and bullying is related to gender because they reinforce differential power status whereby boys, for example, may feel pressure to show dominance, strength, or control. Girls may be judged for appearance or behavior that challenges traditional expectations. The most vulnerable children and adolescents, including those who are poor or from ethnic, linguistic or cultural minorities or migrant or refugee communities or have disabilities, are at higher risk of school violence and bullying. Children and adolescents whose sexual orientation, gender identity or expression does not conform to traditional social or gender norms are also disproportionately affected.

## Activity 1: Exploring Power (20 minutes)

6. Ask participants to sit back down in the circle and ask the following questions:

- When you were the Persons, how did you treat your Things? How did it feel to treat them as an object?
- What bad things can happen in school when we treat others like Things (e.g. anger and violence such as bullying in school, feeling depressed or disconnected from school)?

## Activity 2: Emanuel's Story  (20 minutes)

9. Say that in the last activity, learners learned how power is used by people to bully others. Use the key points below to expand upon this.

## Gender and Bullying Behavior

- Bullying happens when there is an imbalance of power or strength between people. Many times, bullying is used to control others and show that they are strong or powerful.
- Boys, girls, and all children regardless of gender identity can experience bullying in and out of school.
- There is no one specific cause for bullying. Rather, bullying happens because of multiple causes put together like unequal power between groups, the wider school environment, and what we learn in the wider world around us.
- Many people think that boys are 'naturally' violent - that it is in their biology. But this is not true. Violence is a learned behavior.
- -For example, a boy may have seen his father use violence against his mother when he was a child and learned that this is a normal way to treat a partner in a relationship.
- If violence is learned then it can also be un-learned.
- People who are bullied have the right to receive support and be helped to get out of the situation.
- People who bully also need support to understand their harmful behavior and stop it. Often, those who bully are acting out of pain or problems they don't know how to deal with.

## Setting Up the Space (Do this for all Lessons!)

## Lesson Plan 10: My Academic Support System

## Objective

In this last Lesson, learners will celebrate how far they've come throughout the year in understanding how transforming gender norms will help them to succeed academically, how they've acquired new skills about how to do well in school, and gained stronger friendships.

## Total Lesson Plan Time: 1 hour

Activity 1: My Lessons Learned (20 minutes)

Activity 2: My Support Network (20 minutes)

Activity 3: Celebration! (20 minutes)

## Recommended Materials

- Flipchart paper
- Markers
- Copies of the handout 'My Support Network' for all youth
- Paper awards or other forms of recognition

## Activity 1: My Lessons Learned (20 minutes)

1. Tell the learners that these next activities will help them to reflect back on all that they have all learned as a group. Say that it is important to reflect on what they have learned, and how they have grown.
2. Divide the learners into groups of 4 to 5.
3. Ask them to share in their group 1 thing they learned that will help them succeed in school, and 1 thing they will share with a friend. They will have about 10 minutes to discuss.
4. Walk around to each group and listen.
5. When groups are done, ask for 2-3 groups to volunteer to share what they learned and what they will share with a friend.
6. Thank them by saying they have learned so much throughout the school year. They are very smart and should feel proud of all that they have accomplished together.
7. Say that you have also learned a lot from them. Share any favorite memories you have from these lessons or any moments that made you think proud of your learners.

## Activity 2: My Support Network (20 minutes)

8. Tell the learners they will now think about all the people in their Support Network these are people who you can count on as they continue to progress in school and beyond.
9. Give all participants the handout, 'My Support Network.'
10. Draw your own 'Support Network' on large flipchart paper using the figure below as a guide.

## Circle Closest to the Center

11. Ask the participants: Who is the first person you can go to when you feel sad, angry, or just want to talk about a problem?
12. Write those people in the circle closest to the center.
13. On their individual handout, tell the learners to write down the name of the first person they would go to when they have a problem in the circle closest to the center.

## Circle 2nd Closest to the Center

14. Ask the participants: If that first person is not available, who is the second person you might go to when you have a problem?
15. Write those people in the circle second closest to the center.
16. On their handout, tell the youth to write down the name of the second person they would go to (if the first person was unavailable) when they have a problem, and write down that person's name in the circle second closest to the center.

## Circle 3rd Closest to the Center

17. Ask the group: If the first and second people are not available, who is the third person you might go to when you have a problem?
18. Write those people in the outermost circle.
19. On their handout, tell the learners to write down the name of the third person they would go to (if the first and second person was unavailable) when they have a problem, and write down that person's name in the circle third closest to the center.
20. Say, 'Congratulations! You just created a support network! A support network is a group of people who care about you, and who you can reach out to when you are having a hard time in school and in life.'
21. Ask the learners, what is good about having a support network like this? How can you let these people know you want them in your support network?
